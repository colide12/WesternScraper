              Synopsis

              Background: Patentee brought action against competitor alleging infringement of two patents for marine engine exhaust systems. Following a jury verdict for patentee, the United States District Court for the District of Massachusetts, Nathaniel M. Gorton, J., denied competitor's motion for judgment as a matter of law and denied patentee's motion for permanent injunction. Competitor appealed and patentee cross-appealed.
              Holdings: The Court of Appeals, Moore, Circuit Judge, held that:
              1 substantial evidence supported jury's presumed finding that a nexus existed between the objective evidence of non-obviousness of patents and merits of claimed invention;
              2 substantial evidence supported jury's presumed finding that patents solved long-felt need for reduction in carbon monoxide emissions from marine engines;
              3 substantial evidence supported jury's presumed finding of industry praise for patentee's claimed invention;
              4 substantial evidence supported jury's presumed factual finding that there was skepticism in the industry that low-carbon monoxide marine generators could be produced;
              5 substantial evidence supported jury's presumed finding that competitor copied patentee's claimed invention;
              6 substantial evidence supported jury's presumed finding that objective evidence of non-obviousness of patented invention outweighed evidence of obviousness; and
              7 district court did not abuse its discretion in enhancing damages.
              Affirmed in part, vacated in part, and remanded.

              Background
              Westerbeke Corporation1 and Kohler are competitors who manufacture and sell marine generators (â€œgen-setsâ€) that are used on houseboats to create electrical power for appliances such as refrigerators and air conditioners. Gen-sets have two main parts, an engine and a generator. The exhaust from a typical engine in a gen-set, like any gasoline-powered engine, contains carbon monoxide, which can cause asphyxiation at certain concentrations. Carbon monoxide is particularly dangerous on boats, where the living quarters are confined in close proximity to the engine. Prior to the invention of the patents in suit, the exhaust pipes of prior art marine gen-sets were vented out of the boat into a safe location to reduce potential exposure to carbon monoxide. In the early 2000s, the National Institute for Occupational Safety and Health (â€œNIOSHâ€) investigated concerns about carbon-monoxide-related poisonings and deaths on houseboats. NIOSH found that, for houseboats with gen-sets that discharged exhaust around the swim platform, carbon monoxide levels in the swim area were at or above levels that are immediately dangerous to life and health.
              The '044 and '196 patents, which claim priority to 2003 and have similar specifications, are directed to marine engine exhaust systems that reduce the amount of carbon monoxide released in the exhaust. See '044 patent at Abstract, 1:13â€“14, 1:47â€“58, 2:12â€“30. The Background sections of the patents discuss the use of chemical catalysts as â€œ[s]ome of the most effective and cost-efficient emissions controlsâ€ and discuss that it was generally known that these catalysts work better at higher temperatures. Id. at 1:21â€“27. They note that most of the development work for exhaust catalysts focused on catalytic converters in automotive applications. But they explain that marine gen-sets are subject to different regulations than automotive engines, including regulations for emissions and safety. Id. at 1:27â€“32. One such regulation requires that exposed engine and exhaust system surface temperatures be kept low to reduce fire hazard potential. Id. at 1:32â€“35. The specifications explain that typical marine engines inject seawater into exhaust flows to cool exhaust gases and frequently circulate seawater through exhaust system components to keep surface temperatures low. Id. at 1:35â€“39.

              [A] an exhaust system including
              [B] a catalyst cooled by a flow of coolant, [C] the catalyst arranged to intercept a flow of exhaust;
              [D] a coolant injector that injects coolant into the flow of exhaust at a point downstream of the catalyst; and
              [E] a sensor arranged to sense a characteristic of the flow of exhaust; and

              B. Objective Considerations
              The objective indicia of non-obviousness play an important role as a guard against the statutorily proscribed hindsight reasoning in the obviousness analysis.               78Kohler asserts on appeal that objective considerations of non-obviousness can never overcome a strong prima facie case of obviousness. Kohler misperceives the obviousness inquiry. A determination of whether a patent claim is invalid as obvious under Â§ 103 requires consideration of all four Graham factors, and it is error to reach a conclusion of obviousness until all those factors are considered. In re Cyclobenzaprine Hydrochloride Extendedâ€“Release Capsule Patent Litig., 676 F.3d 1063, 1075 (Fed. Cir. 2012) (citing Richardsonâ€“Vicks Inc. v. Upjohn Co., 122 F.3d 1476, 1483 (Fed. Maersk Drilling USA, Inc., 699 F.3d 1340, 1349 (Fed. Thus, the strength of each of the Graham factors must be weighed in every case and must be weighted en route to the final determination of obviousness or non-obviousness.

              2. Long-Felt Need
              17Evidence of a long felt but unresolved need tends to show non-obviousness because it is reasonable to infer that the need would have not persisted had the solution been obvious.               4. Skepticism

              III. Willful Infringement
              39This case was decided by the district court under the then-applicable willful infringement standard. At the time of this decision, proof of willful infringement required â€œclear and convincing evidence that the infringer acted despite an objectively high likelihood that its actions constituted infringement of a valid patentâ€ and that â€œthis objectively-defined risk ... was either known or so obvious that it should have been known.â€ In re Seagate Tech., LLC, 497 F.3d 1360, 1371 (Fed.               Kohler argues that the judgment of willful infringement must be reversed for two independent reasons. Pulse Electronics, Inc., 769 F.3d 1371 (Fed. Cir. 2014), cert. granted, â€“â€“â€“ U.S. â€“â€“â€“â€“, 136 S.Ct. 356, 193 L.Ed.2d 289 (2015), and Bard Peripheral Vascular, Inc. v. W.L. Second, it argues that WBIP did not present evidence to the jury that Kohler was aware of the patents in suit, which is an element of the subjective prong of the willfulness test, such that the jury's verdict that Kohler willfully infringed cannot stand. After this case was argued on appeal, the Supreme Court decided Halo Electronics, Inc. v. Pulse Electronics, Inc., â€“â€“â€“ U.S. â€“â€“â€“â€“, 136 S.Ct. 1923, 195 L.Ed.2d 278 (2016) (â€œHaloâ€).
              4041Under Halo, we review the district court's determination to award enhanced damages under 35 U.S.C. Â§ 284 for abuse of discretion. Although â€œ[t]he Seagate test reflects, in many respects, a sound recognition that enhanced damages are generally appropriate under Â§ 284 only in egregious cases,â€ the Supreme Court rejected that test as â€œunduly rigidâ€ and as â€œimpermissibly encumber[ing] the statutory grant of discretion to district courts.â€ Id. at 1932.
              As to Kohler's first argumentâ€”that its defenses were objectively reasonableâ€”the Supreme Court's decision in Halo expressly rejected the notion that objective recklessness must be found in every case involving enhanced damages for willful infringement. The Court cited the objective recklessness requirement as the â€œprincipal problem with Seagate's two-part test,â€ explaining that â€œ[s]uch a threshold requirement excludes from discretionary punishment many of the most culpable offenders,â€ including those â€œwho intentionally infringe[ ] another's patent.â€ Halo, 136 S.Ct. at 1932.               42In this case, the district court determined that Kohler was objectively reckless under Seagate, as its litigation-developed obviousness and non-infringement defenses were unreasonable.11 WBIP, 2014 WL 585854, at *5. Knowledge of the patent alleged to be willfully infringed continues to be a prerequisite to enhanced damages. See Halo, 136 S.Ct. at 1932â€“33 (discussing knowledge requirement for intent). We do not interpret Halo as changing the established law that the factual components of the willfulness question should be resolved by the jury.13 See Richardson v. Suzuki Motor Co., 868 F.2d 1226, 1250 (Fed. Cir. 1989) (â€œAbsent sufficient basis for directing the verdict, Richardson has the right of jury determination of this factual question. Willfulness of behavior is a classical jury question of intent. When trial is had to a jury, the issue should be decided by the jury.â€ (citations omitted)); Halo Elecs., Inc. v. Pulse Elecs., Inc., 769 F.3d 1371, 1386 (Fed. Cir. 2014) (O'Malley, J., concurring) (â€œ[W]e have long held that a willfulness determination contains issues of fact that should be submitted to a jury.â€).
              Kohler does not contest that it, in fact, had pre-suit knowledge of the patents in suit. And in fact, Kohler admitted in its Undisputed Statement of Facts in support of its Motion for Summary Judgment of Non-Infringement that it â€œfirst became aware of the '044 Patent at the latest by August 20, 2010â€ when it received an inquiry â€œregarding its knowledge of any WBIP patents on low carbon emission marine generator products.â€ J.A. 2880. Despite this admission, Kohler argues that no evidence of its knowledge was presented to the jury and thus the jury's finding of willfulness should be rejected because â€œthere was no basis to findâ€”or even inferâ€”that Kohler knew or should have known of an objectively high risk of patent infringement.â€ Appellant's Br. 70â€“71.
              We conclude that there was substantial evidence for the jury's finding that Kohler had knowledge of the patents in suit at the time of infringement. At trial, WBIP presented *1342 testimony from John (â€œJackâ€) Westerbeke, the inventor and majority-owner of Westerbeke Corporation, that Westerbeke's lowâ€“carbon monoxide gen-sets have been marked with the patents in suit since their issuance. Supporting this testimony, WBIP submitted an email Mr. Westerbeke sent the day after the '044 patent issued in 2008, which states â€œI have to get a patent label on all gas EFI products immediatelyâ€ and that the label would say â€œU.S. Pat. No. 7,314,044; Other patents pending.â€ J.A. 17,192. WBIP submitted representative photographs of Westerbeke's lowâ€“carbon monoxide gen-sets that are clearly marked with both patents in suit. J.A. 17,261. It also presented testimony that Westerbeke and Kohler were the only two companies in the market that provide lowâ€“carbon monoxide gen-sets, and documentary evidence, such as Kohler's November 2004 internal request for funding to develop lowâ€“carbon monoxide gen-sets, demonstrating that Kohler was aware of Westerbeke patents covering Westerbeke's Safe-CO gen-sets. The district court also had before it Kohler's admission in its Statement of Undisputed Facts in support of its Motion for Summary Judgment of Non-Infringement that Kohler â€œfirst became aware of the '044 Patent at the latest by August 20, 2010â€ when it received an inquiry â€œregarding its knowledge of any WBIP patents on low carbon emission marine generator products.â€ J.A. 2880. The jury had record evidence upon which it could have inferred that Kohler had knowledge of the patents at issue, and thus its finding is supported by substantial evidence.
              48Consistent with Halo, the district court, exercising its discretion, decided not to treble damages, but rather to enhance damages by 50%. The district court has the discretion to decide whether the case is sufficiently egregious to warrant enhancing damages and to decide the amount of enhancement that is warranted (up to the statutory limit of treble damages). And the Court explained, â€œnone of this is to say that enhanced damages must follow a finding of egregious misconduct.â€ Halo, 136 S.Ct. at 1933. We review the district court's decision to enhance damages under an abuse of discretion standard. Id. at 1934.
              We cannot say that the district court abused its discretion in enhancing damages for Kohler's willful infringement.
              IV. WBIP's Cross-Appeal
              49WBIP cross-appeals the district court's denial of a permanent injunction. The district court originally denied WBIP's motion for a permanent injunction, reasoning the public interest factor weighed against an injunction because, as WBIP was a much smaller producer of lowâ€“carbon monoxide gen-sets than Kohler, it â€œwould deprive the consuming public of access to a potentially life saving product.â€ J.A. 10,381â€“82. It determined that, based on its public interest finding, it need not address the remaining factors identified in eBay Inc. v. MercExchange, LLC, 547 U.S. 388, 391, 126 S.Ct. 1837, 164 L.Ed.2d 641 (2006). WBIP moved for reconsideration, arguing that the district court misunderstood WBIP's manufacturing capacity, which was sufficient to manufacture generators for Kohler's customers. The district court declined to reconsider its denial of a permanent injunction, stating that â€œ[e]ven if [WBIP] has a larger manufacturing capability than previously estimated, the Court is persuaded that it is in the public interest to have more than one company manufacture lowâ€“carbon monoxide generatorsâ€ such that an ongoing royalty was a â€œmore appropriate solution.â€ J.A. 10,671.
              50On appeal, WBIP argues that the district court erred in its consideration of the eBay factors. We agree that the district *1343 court's analysis is sufficiently flawed to constitute an abuse of discretion warranting vacating the judgment. Before the district court WBIP argued, inter alia, that there is a â€œpublic interest to uphold patent rights.â€ J.A. 8127. But the district court did not explain how this public interest was outweighed by the public interest of having more than one manufacturer of gen-sets that produce lowâ€“carbon monoxide in their exhaust, especially if WBIP does have the manufacturing capacity to meet the industry's needs. The district court's decision is based on its reasoning that having more manufacturers of a lifesaving good in the market is better for the public interest. But this reasoning is true in nearly every situation involving such goods, such that, if it alone is sufficient, it would create a categorical rule denying permanent injunctions for life-saving goods, such as many patented pharmaceutical products. As the Supreme Court has warned, categorical rules regarding permanent injunctions are disfavored. See eBay, 547 U.S. at 394, 126 S.Ct. 1837 (â€œJust as the District Court erred in its categorical denial of injunctive relief, the Court of Appeals erred in its categorical grant of such relief.â€). And Congress has expressly indicated that injunctions may be granted in cases involving lifesaving goods, such as pharmaceutical drugs. See 35 U.S.C. Â§ 271(e)(4)(B) (â€œ[I]njunctive relief may be granted against an infringer to prevent the commercial manufacture, use, offer to sell, or sale within the United States or importation into the United States of an approved drug, veterinary biological product, or biological product.â€). In denying WBIP a permanent injunction on these grounds, the district court abused its discretion. We note that the district court limited its analysis to the public interest factor alone and that its decision to deny an injunction cannot be affirmed on this basis in light of this record. We vacate its judgment and remand for the district court to conduct a more thorough analysis of the eBay factors in the first instance.
              Conclusion
              For the foregoing reasons, we affirm the district court's denial of judgment as a matter of law that the asserted claims would have been obvious and lack sufficient written description and the willful infringement determination. We vacate the district court's denial of WBIP's motion for a permanent injunction, and remand for further consideration.
